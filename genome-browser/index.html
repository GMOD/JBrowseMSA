In the genome browser

react-msaview ships as a JBrowse 2 plugin (jbrowse-plugin-msaview), so the same viewer — alignment, phylogenetic tree, and all — runs as a view inside JBrowse, stacked alongside a Linear Genome View with gene annotation and multiple-alignment tracks. Every view here is opened by a declarative link: the whole multi-view session lives in the URL, with nothing to install or set up.

A combined view: factor XII lost in whales

The link below opens one JBrowse view with two panels. On top, a Linear Genome View of the human F12 locus (coagulation factor XII) showing the NCBI RefSeq gene track and a UCSC Multiz mammalian DNA alignment. Below, the react-msaview panel: the F12 coding alignment across mammals with its inferred tree.

F12 is intact across land mammals and in the semi-aquatic hippopotamus, but is disabled by frameshifts and premature stop codons in cetaceans — whales, dolphins and porpoises — one of many genes lost in the transition to fully aquatic life. The tree groups the cetaceans together (with hippo as their closest relative), and a single shared frameshift is visible in the alignment. It is exactly the cross-species, nucleotide-level evidence an MSA editor would let you curate and lift over to many species.

A linked protein view: SRC, beside its gene

The combined view above is two independent panels. The MSA viewer can also be connected to the genome view so the two share coordinates. This link opens the human SRC proto-oncogene on hg38 beside the Src-family kinase alignment (with its tree), connected through the SRC transcript model:

  • Click a residue in the alignment → the genome view navigates to that residue's codon.
  • Hover the gene in the genome view → the corresponding alignment column highlights.

The mapping runs through the codon structure of the transcript, so it is a true protein-to-nucleotide link rather than a side-by-side approximation. SRC is the prototypical tyrosine-kinase proto-oncogene, with the SH3 / SH2 / kinase domain architecture the family is named for.

Zoomed on a mutation: BRAF V600E

The same connected pattern, opened on a single codon. This link puts the human BRAF proto-oncogene on hg38 beside the RAF-family kinase alignment, with the genome view positioned right on the V600 codon (highlighted in the genome) and the matching alignment column pre-highlighted across every sequence.

V600E — a single c.1799T>A change at chr7:140,753,336 that swaps valine for glutamate — is the most common oncogenic mutation in melanoma and a recurrent driver in colorectal and thyroid cancer, hairy-cell leukemia and others. V600 sits in the kinase activation segment, and the alignment shows why the position matters: the valine is invariant down the RAF family and across human, mouse, chicken and fly, so the highlighted column is solid. Because the views are connected through the BRAF transcript model, the lit alignment column and the highlighted genome base are the very same residue — click the column and the genome stays locked on the V600E codon.

This is the protein the proteinbrowser demos center on, here as a self-contained declarative link with the biology made explicit.

Conservation meets pathogenicity: TP53 R248

The BRAF link shows one mutation; this one shows a whole clinical archive landing on a single conserved column. It opens the human TP53 tumour suppressor on hg38 — with a ClinVar pathogenic-variant track — beside an alignment of p53 across thirteen vertebrates (human to zebrafish), connected and positioned on the R248 codon.

R248 is the single most frequently mutated residue in TP53 across human cancers. It reaches into the DNA minor groove to let p53 bind its targets, so substitutions there abolish function. In the genome view a dense wall of distinct ClinVar pathogenic variants stacks onto the three bases of the R248 codon; in the alignment directly below, the highlighted column reads R in every species — invariant for ~430 million years. The conserved column and the pathogenic pileup are the same position, viewed through evolution and through the clinic at once. (ClinVar is updated weekly, so the exact variant count drifts over time.)

How the links are built

Each link is a JBrowse session spec: ?config=…&session=spec-{"views":[…]}, where each entry launches a view — a LinearGenomeView with its assembly, location and tracks, and an MsaView pointed at an alignment file. To connect the two (the SRC example), the genome view is given an explicit id and the MsaView carries a connectedViewId referencing it, plus a connectedFeature — the transcript model used for the protein↔genome codon mapping — and a querySeqName naming the matching row in the alignment. A highlightColumns array (the BRAF and TP53 examples) seeds the highlighted-column overlay so a link can open with specific columns lit, and extra tracks entries (the TP53 ClinVar track) load alongside the gene track. Those transcript models are derived reproducibly from the public RefSeq annotation by the generators under scripts/ (src-protein-link, braf-protein-link, tp53-protein-link); the BRAF and TP53 directories also carry a build-* script that regenerates the alignment, tree and (for TP53) the ClinVar variant data from their public sources. A view loaded from a cross-origin config asks you to trust the plugin once before it renders.
